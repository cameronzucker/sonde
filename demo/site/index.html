Two real modems · a live connection · a real Watterson channel · not on-air. Each session stands up two real ARDOP stations (ardopcf) and runs an actual connection between them — CONNECT handshake, bandwidth negotiation, and adaptive-rate ARQ — with their audio bridged through the project's hf-channel-sim (a real ITU-R F.520 Watterson ionospheric model: multipath fading + Doppler at the Good/Moderate/Poor/Flutter presets) and streamed to your browser live. No radio is keyed. ARDOP has a link layer, so lost blocks are retransmitted: at a workable SNR the image arrives intact, the modems adapt the rate down as the channel worsens, and below the cliff the connection never closes. ARDOP is a known-good external baseline — the bar Sonde is being built to meet — and is never reimplemented here. (SNR is referenced to a fixed TX level over the full audio band, not yet calibrated to a standard 2.4 kHz noise bandwidth, so treat the dB as relative.)

What you're seeing

  1. Set the channel, then Connect. Drag SNR, pick a condition, choose the ARQ bandwidth ceiling (the widest mode allowed), and press Connect. A session is real airtime — it plays out live over ~30–60 s.
  2. Two real ARDOP stations connect. The backend runs a real CONNECT handshake, negotiates a bandwidth, and transfers a ~3 KB image over adaptive-rate ARQ — their audio bridged through a real ITU-R F.520 Watterson HF channel (hf-channel-sim). Real modulation, real fading, real FEC, real retransmission.
  3. Watch + hear it live. The on-air signal streams to the waterfall (and your speakers) as it transmits; the connection/ARQ log streams the handshake, the negotiated mode, and every rate change; the recon photo lands intact — slower as the rate drops, and not at all once the SNR falls below the connect cliff.
